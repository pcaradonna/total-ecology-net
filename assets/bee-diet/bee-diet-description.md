# On the Diet Diversity of Wild Bumble Bees

## Web text

The northwestern grizzly bear emerges from hibernation in the spring to eat an abundance of buds and flowers, insects and salmon in the summer, and then feasts upon fruit and nuts in the fall. 
The adult greater sage grouse subsists on sagebrush leaves, but the young eat almost exclusively insects. 
Meanwhile, flower-feeding bumble bees navigate a complex and changing floral resource landscape as the needs of the colony extend across the entirety of the growing season. 
The composition and diversity of animal diets are essential parts of their ecology and evolution: from apex predators to insect pollinators, an animal’s diet is shaped by the changing seasons, growth and development, and shifting nutritional needs. 
Thus, characterizing diet diversity requires a life-cycle perspective.

## Extra web text

For most animals, however, our understanding remains coarse and temporally static. 
This limitation is especially pronounced for insects, including important and at-risk pollinators like bumble bees, where the lack of detailed, life stage-specific diet information constrains our ability to link resource use to population dynamics, community interactions, and conservation.
Bumble bees are quintessential generalist foragers: species incorporate many different types of floral resources into their diets over the colony life cycle. 
Descriptions of wild bumble bee floral resource use have been outlined in the literature, but are limited to certain regions, or do not include observations across the entire period of foraging activity. 
These broad-level characterizations may overlook meaningful ecological variation, potentially obscuring diet diversity both within and across species. Knowing species and colony life stage-specific diet requirements is a fundamental step in better understanding bumble bee ecology, and targeted conservation efforts.
